Master’s Degree Projects

Revaluation of “The China Syndrome” (Autor et al.)

Empirical Econometrics I | Grade: 8.5/10 | Maastricht University

A comprehensive replication and critical revaluation of Autor, Dorn, and Hanson’s seminal 2013 paper on the impact of Chinese import competition on U.S. local labor markets.

Key Contributions:

  • Fully replicated the original identification strategy, converting 1,500+ lines of STATA code into R
  • Implemented exposure-robust standard errors following Borusyak et al. (2022) for improved statistical inference
  • Critically assessed instrument relevance (F-statistics > 100) and exogeneity assumptions underlying the shift-share design
  • Extended the empirical specification with five additional models to evaluate potential omitted variable bias, lag structure choices, and fixed-effects design
  • Reassessed statistical inference using clustered and exposure-robust standard errors, discussing implications for significance and economic interpretation
  • Provided a structured methodological critique of the original specification choices and their impact on inferential validity

Impact: Findings aligned with ongoing debates on shift-share identification and exposure-based inference in trade literature. Validated key coefficients 12 years after original publication while extending the analysis timeframe.

Tools: R (Tidyverse, AER, lmtest, sandwich, modelsummary) | Methods: IV estimation, shift-share designs, panel data analysis, exposure-robust inference

West Germany Postwar Growth: Human Capital & Immigration

Macroeconomics I | Grade: 8.5/10 | Maastricht University

An econometric decomposition of West Germany’s remarkable postwar economic recovery, the “Wirtschaftswunder” (1950-1990), with particular focus on the relative contributions of human capital accumulation and immigration to GDP growth.

Key Contributions:

  • Constructed a harmonized historical dataset from 11 archival time series to analyze drivers of the Wirtschaftswunder
  • Developed and calibrated five alternative growth-accounting frameworks beyond the baseline Cobb-Douglas specification
  • Implemented modern calibration procedures in Julia (1,000+ lines of code) to infer latent investment, human capital, and migration dynamics not directly observed in available data
  • Compared explanatory power across model structures, highlighting the relative contribution of immigration and human capital accumulation to postwar growth
  • Results offer quantitative insight into long-run growth dynamics in postwar reconstruction contexts

Impact: Provides new empirical evidence on the drivers of economic miracles, with implications for development economics and migration policy.

Tools: Julia (DataFrames.jl, GLM.jl), PlutoNotebook | Methods: Growth accounting, productivity decomposition, historical data analysis, calibration techniques
