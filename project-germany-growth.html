West Germany Postwar Growth

Course: Macroeconomics I | Grade: 8.5/10 | Institution: Maastricht University

An analytical exploration of the macroeconomic drivers behind the 'Wirtschaftswunder', focusing on the dynamics of human capital and immigration between 1950 and 1990.

Project Overview

This project investigates the rapid economic recovery of West Germany following World War II. Specifically, the study assesses whether the standard Neoclassical Growth Model (NGM) can explain this postwar growth by providing a growth-accounting decomposition that attributes GDP per capita growth to each factor of production. The paper isolates the crucial roles played by the mobilization of human capital and the influx of migrant workers in fueling sustained macroeconomic growth and counterbalancing war-damaged physical capital.

Technical Approach

The research synthesizes historical data with macroeconomic growth models, evaluating five distinct specifications of the Cobb-Douglas production function. The analysis integrates data from the Penn World Table, the Deutsche Bundesbank, and EU KLEMS to construct consistent series for productive capital stock and factor-income shares. The models explicitly account for skill heterogeneity via a wage-premium measure and test the validity of treating human capital as a distinct input versus a labor-augmenting factor. The computational work and growth decompositions were executed using Julia.

Tools: Julia, Neoclassical Growth Models, Growth-Accounting Decomposition, Perpetual-Inventory Method.
